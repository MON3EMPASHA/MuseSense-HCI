\title{MuseSense: A Multimodal Tangible Museum Guide for 3D Artifacts, Narration, and Vision-Based Personalization}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{MuseSense HCI Project Team}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
MuseSense is a prototype museum guide that combines tangible markers, computer vision, 3D artifact presentation, and narrated storytelling into a single interactive experience. The system uses reacTIVision/TUIO markers for direct artifact selection, a Python vision service for face-based session recognition and background sensing, and a C\# WinForms client for 3D model rendering, audio narration, favorites management, and visitor-facing interaction. The implementation is split across two coordinated projects: the main museum application and a supporting background service for gesture and Bluetooth-based identity handling. This paper documents the design rationale, software architecture, interaction flow, and prototype validation of the current build. It also situates MuseSense within the literature on embodied interaction, tangible interfaces, public displays, museum HCI, vision-based perception, and gesture recognition.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
museum HCI, tangible interaction, TUIO, reacTIVision, MediaPipe, face recognition, 3D rendering, narration, embodied interaction
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
Museums increasingly rely on digital systems to enrich artifact exploration, but many installations still force visitors to choose between expressive physical interaction and structured screen-based navigation. MuseSense addresses this gap by turning museum browsing into a multimodal experience: visitors place tangible markers on an interactive surface to open artifacts, the system identifies users through vision-based login, and the desktop client presents 3D models, metadata, and narration audio in a responsive guided interface.

The current project is not a generic demo shell. It is a working prototype with a full implementation stack: Python for camera capture, face recognition, and socket communication; C\# for desktop rendering, TUIO marker handling, and audio playback; and a separate gesture-oriented service that preserves the original gesture-research direction of the project. The paper has two goals. First, it documents the actual system that was built. Second, it explains how the implementation fits established HCI research on tangible interaction, embodied interfaces, public displays, and museum narration.

This paper makes four contributions. First, it presents an integrated museum prototype that combines marker-based tangible interaction, face-based personalization, three-dimensional artifact presentation, and narration playback in a unified visitor workflow. Second, it documents a distributed software architecture that separates perception and rendering concerns while preserving interaction continuity through explicit state synchronization. Third, it formalizes a state-driven interaction model that governs artifact selection, session binding, and favorites persistence in a shared public environment. Fourth, it provides a literature-grounded argument for why this embodied interaction strategy is appropriate for cultural heritage settings where social visibility, interpretive context, and ease of entry are central design constraints.

\section{Literature Review}
\subsection{Embodied and Tangible Interaction}
MuseSense builds on the classic argument that interaction should be understood through the body, physical space, and the meaning of artifacts rather than only through command syntax. Ishii and Ullmer's Tangible Bits vision framed physical objects as interfaces to digital information, showing how graspable artifacts can link the physical and virtual worlds in a more legible way than conventional windows-and-icons interfaces~\cite{ishii1997}. Dourish extended this direction by arguing that embodied interaction is not merely a richer input method, but a shift in how meaning is produced through situated action, physical engagement, and social context~\cite{dourish2001}.

Hornecker and Buur operationalized these ideas for tangible interaction by showing that TUI design must account for both physical manipulation and social coordination in shared spaces~\cite{horneckerbuur2006}. MuseSense follows this logic closely: the table marker is not only a selector, but also a social cue that invites visitors to manipulate a real object in front of others. That visible, embodied act is part of the experience rather than a side effect.

\subsection{Public Displays and Museums}
Museums are public settings, so the system must work not only as a technical interface but also as a socially understandable one. Brignull and Rogers showed that public displays attract attention when the interaction is visible and easy to interpret, while Muller et al. argued that public interfaces should be designed around entry, engagement, and return-to-passive states rather than assuming a single uninterrupted user~\cite{brignull2003,muller2010}. Reeves et al. further emphasized the spectator experience, noting that people who are not directly interacting still shape the meaning and social comfort of the interaction~\cite{reeves2005}.

Museums specifically have a long tradition of tangible and embodied interaction research. Hornecker and Stifter observed that interactive museum installations benefit from embodied facilitation, where the physical configuration of the exhibit helps visitors discover what to do without reading long instructions~\cite{horneckerstifter2006}. More recently, museum-HCI work has emphasized accessibility, narrative presentation, and multisensory interpretation as part of a richer visitor experience~\cite{pietroni2021}. MuseSense aligns with that direction by pairing artifact browsing with narration audio and personal favorites.

\subsection{Computer Vision and Gesture Recognition}
MuseSense uses vision not as a novelty, but as an infrastructure layer. MediaPipe provides a lightweight pipeline model for perception tasks that need to run in real time on commodity hardware~\cite{lugaresi2019}. BlazePose extends that idea with on-device real-time body pose tracking, making pose-based interaction feasible in interactive demos and mobile settings~\cite{bazarevsky2020}. In parallel, face recognition in MuseSense relies on a standard embedding-based approach. FaceNet demonstrated that face images can be mapped to a compact Euclidean embedding where distance corresponds to identity similarity, which fits the local identity model used in this project~\cite{schroff2015}. The Python backend also uses a face detector, which is consistent with the cascaded face-detection family introduced by Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2016}.

The gesture side of the project is informed by recognizers that avoid heavy training requirements. Wobbrock et al. showed that a simple template recognizer can be effective for prototype interfaces~\cite{wobbrock2007}, Anthony and Wobbrock extended this direction to multistroke gestures~\cite{anthony2010}, and Vatavu et al. proposed the \$P recognizer for point-cloud gesture comparison~\cite{vatavu2012}. MuseSense uses that family of ideas in the background gesture service that supports motion-driven interaction experiments.

Social acceptability matters as much as recognition accuracy. Rico and Brewster showed that gestures can feel more or less appropriate depending on context and visibility~\cite{rico2010}, and Hoff and Bashir argued that repeated failures degrade trust in automation even when the system later recovers~\cite{hoff2015}. Those findings are directly relevant to a museum guide, because visitors will abandon the interface quickly if the system feels unstable or unpredictable.

\subsection{Narration and Evaluation}
Narration is not an accessory in MuseSense; it is part of the interpretive layer that turns an artifact into a story. In museum HCI, audio and narrative are often used to add context without crowding the visual interface, and evaluation is usually framed around workload, clarity, and visitor engagement rather than raw task speed~\cite{pietroni2021}. Because MuseSense is still a prototype, the present paper focuses on architecture and scenario-based validation, while keeping formal user-study methods such as NASA-TLX and thematic analysis available for future evaluation designs~\cite{hart1988,braun2006}.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{HCI Overview Diagram and Architecture}
MuseSense is implemented as a distributed local system rather than a monolithic desktop application. The end-to-end human-computer interaction flow is summarized in the full-width overview diagram in Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}. The diagram is intentionally organized around interaction semantics rather than code structure, so that perception, interpretation, interface response, and content delivery can be read as one continuous visitor journey.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{paper/system_architecture.jpeg}
\caption{MuseSense HCI overview diagram. The overview presents the complete interaction loop across sensing, state interpretation, tangible input handling, visual rendering, narration delivery, and preference persistence.}
\label{fig:overview}
\end{figure*}

At the perception boundary, camera and marker streams capture the visitor's embodied actions: face presence and identity cues support session binding, while marker identity and pose support direct artifact selection and manipulation. These signals are transformed into compact interaction-state events that are shared with the presentation client. This event-oriented exchange separates sensing latency from interface responsiveness, which is critical in public settings where short response delays can quickly degrade user confidence.

At the presentation boundary, the desktop client resolves each state event into user-facing outcomes: artifact context loading, model rendering, explanatory metadata, narration playback, and favorites updates. In HCI terms, the architecture enforces a tight perception-action loop: visitor action modifies system state, state drives interface feedback, and feedback informs subsequent visitor action. The auxiliary gesture pathway is positioned as an extensible modality within the same loop, enabling future multimodal expansion without destabilizing the primary tangible workflow.

At an implementation level, the perception service is responsible for camera processing, identity inference, and inter-process messaging, while the desktop client is responsible for interaction orchestration and visual presentation. Artifact descriptors, user-preference records, and narration assets are managed through a local content layer to ensure robust operation in environments with limited or intermittent network access.

\subsection{Interaction State Flow}
The user experience follows a simple state machine. Visitors begin in a passive browsing state, move into a recognized user session when the camera identifies a known face, and then transition into an artifact-focused state when a TUIO marker is placed on the table. The state logic is visualized in Fig.~\ref{fig:stateflow}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{paper/sate_flow.jpeg}
\caption{MuseSense state flow. The prototype moves between idle browsing, face-based session binding, artifact selection, narration playback, and favorites management.}
\label{fig:stateflow}
\end{figure}

This flow intentionally keeps the interface simple. A marker opens an artifact. A recognized user unlocks personalized actions. A favorites list is attached to the active user. When the face disappears, the session is cleared so the kiosk does not remain bound to the previous visitor.

\subsection{Python Vision and Identity Layer}
The perception service combines OpenCV capture, MediaPipe Holistic, face recognition, and socket output. It reads camera frames from either a local webcam or a network stream, applies configurable preprocessing (including resolution adaptation and orientation correction), and performs identity checks on a throttled schedule to control computational cost. When a known face is recognized, the service sends a compact state message to the desktop client; when the face is lost, it emits a reset signal.

In addition to this primary pipeline, the system includes an auxiliary gesture-processing path based on pose landmarks and template matching. In the current prototype, this module is treated as an experimental extension rather than the principal control channel. This architectural choice preserves interaction stability for public deployment while retaining a clear integration path for future multimodal expansion.

\subsection{C\# Museum Client}
The C\# application is implemented as a WinForms desktop client. It listens to TUIO object events, translates marker identifiers into artifact indices, and renders the selected artifact using a software rasterization pipeline with object loading, texture handling, z-buffering, and frame caching. The artifact detail view also supports narration playback and favorites management.

This client is not a GPU demo. It is a CPU-driven renderer, which matters for the paper because it explains the project's constraints and design choices. The renderer precomputes or caches rotated views and simplifies repeated drawing work where possible. That implementation strategy is appropriate for a local museum installation where predictable behavior matters more than maximum visual fidelity.

\subsection{Artifact, Narration, and Favorites Pipeline}
Artifacts are defined in JSON and bound to TUIO IDs. Each record stores metadata such as name, era, origin, description, model path, audio path, and display color. When a marker is placed, the matching artifact record is loaded, and the 3D model and narration are displayed together. The narration clips are stored as audio files and are played back from the desktop client when the artifact view opens or when the visitor requests it.

Favorites are persisted locally per user. This design keeps the system self-contained and avoids a network dependency during a demo. It also matches the museum setting, where the active user should be able to save and revisit artifacts with minimal friction.

\subsection{TUIO Marker Input}
MuseSense uses the TUIO protocol and reacTIVision to track fiducial markers on the table. The vision tracker emits symbol IDs and pose information over UDP, and the desktop application uses those IDs to load the corresponding artifact. Marker orientation is preserved so the physical manipulation of the object contributes to the digital view. This is the most visible example of embodied interaction in the system: the visitor moves a physical marker and sees the museum content respond.

\section{Experiments}
Because the project is currently at a prototype stage, the evaluation strategy was designed as structured scenario-based validation rather than a full human-subjects study. The objective was to determine whether the integrated system could sustain the core museum interaction loop under realistic operating conditions, including session entry, artifact loading, media navigation, preference persistence, and return-to-idle behavior in a shared kiosk context.

The protocol used repeated end-to-end trials on a local deployment where camera input, marker tracking, perception services, and the desktop client were executed concurrently. Each trial assessed semantic correctness of state transitions, consistency of cross-process synchronization, and continuity of visitor-facing media presentation. Operationally, successful execution required that marker placement trigger the intended artifact context, marker pose updates remain visually coherent, recognized users be bound to the active session, session state clear when identity was no longer observable, narration behavior remain aligned with artifact context, and preference records remain recoverable across interaction cycles. The auxiliary gesture module was evaluated separately for event emission stability and message interpretability.

This protocol prioritizes internal validity for system integration claims. It does not establish population-level usability effects, but it does provide a defensible engineering baseline for subsequent controlled user studies.

\section{Results and Discussion}
The prototype demonstrates the core interaction loop that the system was designed to support. Marker events consistently map to artifact context, the desktop client preserves selected-object state across user actions, and the rendering pipeline presents three-dimensional models alongside textual metadata and narration in a synchronized manner. The state model in Fig.~\ref{fig:stateflow} therefore reflects implemented behavior rather than conceptual aspiration.

The face-recognition layer provides user binding and session reset behavior that is appropriate for a shared kiosk. In practice, this means the system can personalize the favorites list and avoid keeping one visitor's state active after they leave. That is important in a museum because the interface must remain understandable when different people approach it in sequence.

The auxiliary gesture subsystem is also important to interpret correctly. It captures the multimodal ambition of the broader design and provides a reusable recognition path, but it is not yet the primary control modality for the museum interface. This should be understood as a scope boundary rather than a system defect: the finalized interaction path emphasizes robustness for public-facing operation, while the gesture path remains an extensible research component.

Three limitations delimit the current evidence. First, the rendering pipeline is CPU-bound, which may constrain scalability as scene complexity increases. Second, gesture-to-interface coupling remains partial, leaving part of the multimodal interaction hypothesis untested at the interface level. Third, identity personalization currently depends on local records and does not yet implement a complete consent-and-governance workflow. These constraints are non-trivial for deployment, but they do not invalidate the prototype's central contribution: it demonstrates a coherent embodied interaction architecture that couples tangible input, adaptive session logic, and narrative media delivery in a single operational system.

From an academic perspective, the immediate implication is methodological. The present study supports a claim of architectural feasibility and interaction coherence, not a claim of comparative user superiority over touchscreen or audio-guide baselines. A publish-ready next phase should therefore employ a mixed-method design with controlled tasks, workload and trust instruments, and qualitative coding of social interaction behavior in co-located museum use. Within that trajectory, the current prototype serves as a stable experimental platform rather than an endpoint.

\section{Conclusion}
MuseSense is a multimodal museum guide that combines tangible interaction, vision-based identity, three-dimensional artifact rendering, and narration into a coherent prototype. This paper has documented the implemented architecture, clarified the role of perception and presentation components, formalized the TUIO-driven interaction state model, and situated the approach in prior work on embodied interaction, public displays, museum HCI, and real-time perception pipelines.

The next development steps are straightforward: wire the gesture service into the primary museum actions, add a consent and privacy flow for face-based personalization, and run a formal user study to measure usability, social acceptability, and learning effects. Even in its current state, however, MuseSense already demonstrates that a museum guide can be both physically legible and digitally rich without forcing visitors into a conventional screen-first workflow.
