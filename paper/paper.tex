\title{Gesture-Based Interaction in a Smart Museum Installation:\\
A Study of User Frustration, Adaptation, and Social Context Effects}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{[Anonymous for Review]}}

\maketitle

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a user study examining how visitors interact with a
gesture-driven smart museum system. Rather than evaluating the system as a
technical artifact, we investigate five human-centered questions: how
readily visitors complete mid-air gestures, how they respond emotionally
and behaviorally to misrecognition events, how they adapt gesture strategies
after a failure, whether the presence of social companions affects confidence
and interaction style, and whether tuning the MediaPipe Holistic detection
confidence threshold (0.5 vs.\ 0.65) yields measurable gains in task
performance and subjective experience. The system under study combines
MediaPipe Holistic pose estimation with the \$P dollar point-cloud
recognizer~\cite{vatavu2012} to classify four full-body gestures---swipe
left, swipe right, zoom in, and zoom out---plus a circular motion to
bookmark exhibits as favorites. Passive user identification is performed
via Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) proximity scanning, and physical tangible
markers are tracked via the TUIO protocol~\cite{kaltenbrunner2007}. A
within-subjects design compares the two detection-confidence conditions;
social context (solo vs.\ accompanied) is a between-subjects factor.
Methodology, stimuli, and measures are presented in full. Results,
discussion, and conclusions will be completed following participant testing.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
gesture interaction, museum HCI, body-pose recognition, \$P recognizer,
MediaPipe, frustration, social context, TUIO, usability
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}

Museums have long struggled to reconcile the desire for immersive visitor
experiences with the physical and aesthetic constraints of gallery spaces.
Traditional input peripherals---keyboards, mice, touchscreens---are often
incompatible with an exhibition context that prizes both the integrity of
artifacts and the continuity of spatial narrative~\cite{hornecker2006}.
Gesture-based and body-driven interfaces have therefore attracted sustained
research interest as a means of enabling natural, untethered navigation
in public spaces~\cite{muller2010}.

Deploying gesture-recognition systems in real-world cultural heritage
environments, however, introduces human-computer interaction challenges
that laboratory-based usability studies rarely capture. Visitors arrive
with no prior training, bring companions whose social presence may
influence individual behavior, and encounter recognition failures they
are entirely unprepared to handle. These conditions motivate a style of
research focused not on whether the technology functions but on how people
actually behave when using it~\cite{reeves2005}.

This paper reports on a user study conducted with \textit{GestureMuseum},
a smart museum installation supporting body-gesture navigation across three
themed exhibition rooms: Ancient Egypt, Imperial China, and Medieval Europe.
The system uses a computer-vision pipeline based on MediaPipe
Holistic~\cite{lugaresi2019} to continuously track wrist landmarks and
classifies accumulated trajectory windows using the \$P dollar
recognizer~\cite{vatavu2012}. User login is performed passively via BLE
proximity scanning, and physical TUIO markers on exhibit surfaces trigger
object-specific augmented overlays via reacTIVision~\cite{kaltenbrunner2007}.

The goal of this study is to investigate five interrelated research
questions about the visitor experience:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{RQ1}: How easily can museum visitors complete mid-air
          gestures to control the system?
    \item \textbf{RQ2}: How do visitors express and report frustration or
          confusion when the system misrecognizes their gestures?
    \item \textbf{RQ3}: How do users adapt their gesture strategies after
          a recognition failure?
    \item \textbf{RQ4}: How does the presence of social companions affect
          interaction confidence and style?
    \item \textbf{RQ5}: Does raising the MediaPipe detection confidence
          threshold from 0.5 to 0.65 produce measurably smoother and more
          satisfying interaction?
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section~\ref{sec:rw}
reviews related work; Section~\ref{sec:system} describes the system;
Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the study methodology; Sections~\ref{sec:results}
through~\ref{sec:conclusion} will be completed after data collection.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:rw}

\subsection{Gesture Interaction in Public and Cultural Spaces}

The display blindness and audience funnel phenomena documented by
M\"{u}ller et al.~\cite{muller2010} established a foundational model of
how passers-by progress from ignoring a public display to actively
engaging with it. Their work demonstrated that interaction design must
account for multiple coexisting roles---bystander, curious observer,
direct user---and that transitioning between these roles is heavily
influenced by the physical conspicuousness of the required input actions.
For gesture-based systems, this has direct implications: large arm
movements lower the threshold for entry-level curiosity but may increase
self-consciousness for committed users.

Brignull and Rogers~\cite{brignull2003} introduced the concept of the
\textit{honeypot effect} in public display research, observing that
watching others interact with a display substantially increased
bystanders' willingness to approach and engage. They found this effect
strongest when the interaction appeared visually legible and socially
unthreatening. Museum installations are a canonical context for this
dynamic: groups visiting together routinely observe and comment on each
other's interaction attempts, creating a layered social interaction that
extends well beyond the individual user.

Hornecker and Stifter~\cite{hornecker2006} examined interactive
installations in science museums and identified \textit{embodied
facilitation}---the way a physical interface affords or inhibits
engagement through its spatial configuration---as a primary determinant
of visitor success. Their findings suggest that full-body gesture
interfaces with unclear affordances may produce high rates of exploratory
but unproductive behavior, particularly among older adults or visitors
with limited prior exposure to computer-based interaction.

\subsection{The Dollar Recognizer Family}

Wobbrock et al.~\cite{wobbrock2007} introduced the \$1 unistroke
recognizer as a fast, training-free alternative to machine-learning
gesture classifiers. The key insight was that a small set of
developer-recorded reference templates, combined with a rotation-invariant
distance metric over resampled point paths, could match the accuracy of
far more complex systems for the limited gesture vocabularies typical
of prototype interfaces. Subsequent work extended this approach:
Anthony and Wobbrock~\cite{anthony2010} introduced \$N for
multi-stroke gestures, while Vatavu, Anthony, and Wobbrock~\cite{vatavu2012}
proposed \$P, which represents gestures as unordered point clouds rather
than ordered paths. The point-cloud representation is particularly
well-suited to full-body gesture trajectories, where arm biomechanics
mean that the precise temporal ordering of trajectory points varies
substantially across users and repetitions.

A known limitation of template-based recognizers relevant to this study
is their sensitivity to the representativeness of the reference template
set. Systems whose templates are recorded by a single developer
systematically underperform for users with different body proportions,
movement speeds, or motor styles~\cite{wobbrock2007}. In GestureMuseum,
all templates were recorded by one developer from MP4 videos using a
custom extraction script; this design choice creates a measurable risk
of systematic recognition bias against users whose natural gesture
patterns diverge from the template author's, which the present study
aims to characterize.

\subsection{Body Pose Estimation with MediaPipe}

Lugaresi et al.~\cite{lugaresi2019} introduced MediaPipe as a
framework for building streaming perception pipelines from modular
graph components. Its on-device efficiency---achieved through
carefully optimized model architectures and hardware-accelerated
inference---has made it one of the most widely adopted computer-vision
toolkits for gesture and pose research. The Holistic solution used in
GestureMuseum combines the BlazePose body landmark model~\cite{bazarevsky2020}
with separate hand and face estimators, producing a unified landmark
graph from a single monocular RGB frame.

The \texttt{min\_detection\_confidence} parameter in MediaPipe Holistic
controls how certain the model must be before emitting a landmark
estimate for a given frame. Lower values increase recall (more frames
yield a landmark estimate) at the cost of precision (some estimates are
noisy or spurious). The original GestureMuseum implementation used the
default value of 0.5; the version under study raises it to 0.65, a
choice informed by empirical observation during system development.
The effect of this change on user experience---as opposed to raw
recognition accuracy---has not been previously evaluated in a
controlled study and constitutes the manipulation in RQ5.

\subsection{Frustration and Error Recovery in Gesture Systems}

User frustration in gesture-based systems arises primarily from three
sources: unexpected non-responses (the system ignores a clearly
intentional gesture), false positives (an unintended action is
triggered by ambient movement), and discoverability failures (the user
does not know which gestures are supported)~\cite{rico2010}. Each
source produces a distinct behavioral response. Non-responses tend to
elicit repetition and gesture amplification; false positives tend to
elicit hesitation and reduced movement amplitude; discoverability
failures produce exploratory thrashing~\cite{hoff2015}.

Hoff and Bashir~\cite{hoff2015} demonstrated that repeated automation
failures significantly degrade trust even when subsequent interactions
succeed, an effect they termed \textit{trust calibration degradation}.
This is particularly consequential in museum contexts where visitors
have a single, time-limited exposure to the system: a sequence of early
misrecognition events may lead visitors to abandon gesture interaction
entirely before the system has had the opportunity to demonstrate
reliable performance.

Rico and Brewster~\cite{rico2010} examined the social acceptability of
mobile gestural input and identified three distinct failure-recovery
strategies: \textit{repetition} (the same gesture is repeated,
sometimes with greater force), \textit{strategy substitution} (an
alternative gesture is attempted), and \textit{help-seeking} (the
user vocalizes confusion or turns to a bystander for assistance).
Whether museum visitors employ the same strategies in a fixed
installation context, and whether companion presence influences
strategy selection, is a central question for RQ3 and RQ4.

\subsection{Social Context and Embodied Interaction}

Reeves et al.~\cite{reeves2005} introduced a framework distinguishing
between the \textit{performer}---the primary interactor---and the
\textit{spectator}---those observing the interaction---and argued that
interaction designers must consider the spectator experience as a
first-class concern. They identified a spectrum from \textit{subtle}
interactions (minimal body movement, socially invisible) to
\textit{spectacular} ones (large body movement, publicly visible) and
noted that the choice of interaction style fundamentally alters the
social dynamics of co-located use.

The GestureMuseum gestures sit firmly at the spectacular end of this
spectrum: swipe gestures require broad horizontal arm sweeps, and the
circle gesture requires a full rotational arm motion. Visitors
performing these actions in the presence of companions are effectively
staging a public performance. Brignull and Rogers~\cite{brignull2003}
found that this kind of visible performance can attract additional
bystanders, amplifying the social pressure on the primary interactor.
Whether this amplified visibility inhibits gesture performance---
particularly among visitors who are less comfortable with public
physical expression---is what RQ4 addresses.

\subsection{Tangible Interaction in Museums}

Kaltenbrunner and Bencina~\cite{kaltenbrunner2007} introduced
reacTIVision as an open-source computer-vision framework for
table-based tangible user interfaces, using fiducial marker tracking
to translate physical object manipulation into digital events via the
TUIO protocol. Museum applications of tangible interaction have been
shown to significantly increase dwell time and collaborative engagement
compared to screen-only interfaces~\cite{hornecker2006}, because the
physical manipulability of objects scaffolds collaborative discussion
and reduces the cognitive load of learning a purely virtual interface.

In GestureMuseum, TUIO markers placed on exhibit objects trigger
object-specific content overlays when detected by the reacTIVision
tracker, providing a complementary interaction modality alongside
the gesture navigation layer. The coexistence of two distinct
interaction modalities---mid-air body gestures and physical tangible
manipulation---creates a multimodal interaction environment whose
usability characteristics are largely unstudied in the museum context.

\section{System Description}
\label{sec:system}

GestureMuseum is a real-time smart museum interaction system deployed
across a multi-room exhibition environment. Its architecture comprises
three coupled components: a Python-based computer-vision and gesture
recognition pipeline, a C\# WinForms display application, and a
TUIO-compatible tangible object tracker. Figure~\ref{fig:arch} provides
a schematic overview.

\subsection{Computer Vision Pipeline}

The Python pipeline (implemented as a Jupyter Notebook) uses OpenCV to
capture frames from a webcam at $480 \times 320$ pixels. Each frame is
processed by \texttt{mp.solutions.holistic.Holistic} with
\texttt{model\_complexity=1} and a configurable
\texttt{min\_detection\_confidence} (0.5 in the baseline condition;
0.65 in the improved condition). From each successfully processed frame,
the screen-space coordinates of the right wrist (landmark 15) and
left wrist (landmark 16) are extracted and appended to a rolling
point buffer:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Wrist landmark extraction and buffering.}]
x = int(results.pose_landmarks.landmark[
    mp_pose.PoseLandmark.RIGHT_WRIST].x * image_width)
y = int(results.pose_landmarks.landmark[
    mp_pose.PoseLandmark.RIGHT_WRIST].y * image_height)
Allpoints.append(Point(x, y, 1))
# repeated for LEFT_WRIST
\end{lstlisting}

Every 30 frames (approximately one second at 30~fps), the accumulated
point buffer is passed to the \$P recognizer, which compares it against
four reference templates (SwipeLeft, SwipeRight, ZoomIn, ZoomOut).
A fifth gesture (Circle, for marking exhibits as favorites) is supported
via a template recorded with the same pipeline. On a successful
recognition, the gesture label is transmitted over a TCP socket to the
display application and the buffer is cleared.

\subsection{User Identification via BLE}

At session startup, the pipeline performs a 5-second Bluetooth Low
Energy scan using the \texttt{bleak} library. The MAC address of the
first detected device is transmitted to the C\# application, which
matches it against a registered visitor profile in a CSV file. Matched
profiles load the visitor's name and photograph, enabling a personalized
greeting and a recommendation feed calibrated to the visitor's room
location.

\subsection{Display Application}

The C\# WinForms display application renders museum content using GDI+
graphics on a full-screen window. A \texttt{page} variable ($0$--$4$)
tracks the current view:

\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Page 0}: Home page with personalized exhibit
          recommendations.
    \item \textbf{Page 1}: Room-specific exhibit browser (Egypt, China,
          or Medieval Europe, depending on the visitor's physical location).
    \item \textbf{Pages 2--4}: Visitor information slideshows for rooms
          the visitor is not currently occupying (slides cycle every
          3~seconds).
\end{itemize}

SwipeRight and SwipeLeft commands increment or decrement the page index,
with room-specific skipping logic so that a visitor physically inside a
given room does not receive the slideshow for that same room. The
application simultaneously runs a TuioClient on port 3333; when a
physical marker is detected, its SymbolID selects a background image and
an overlay object rendered at the marker's tracked orientation.

\subsection{Template Creation}

Gesture templates were constructed using a separate script
(\texttt{template\_maker.py}) that processes MP4 video recordings. For
each frame, wrist landmarks are extracted via MediaPipe and written as
\texttt{dollarpy} Point objects to a Python source file. Because all
templates were recorded by a single developer, there is a known risk of
systematic recognition bias against users whose gesture style, body
proportions, or movement speed differ from those of the recording author.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Study Design}

The study uses a mixed-methods design combining quantitative performance
measures with qualitative behavioral observation and semi-structured
interviewing. Two within-subjects factors are manipulated: (1)
\textit{algorithm condition} (baseline: confidence 0.5 vs.\ improved:
confidence 0.65) and (2) \textit{gesture type} (SwipeLeft, SwipeRight,
ZoomIn, ZoomOut, Circle). A between-subjects factor captures
\textit{social context}: whether the participant interacts alone or in
the presence of accompanying companions.

Within-subjects assignment for the algorithm comparison controls for
individual differences in gesture style; each participant serves as
their own control. Condition order is counterbalanced using a Latin
square to mitigate learning and fatigue effects. Social context cannot
be within-subjects because participants cannot be randomly assigned to
bring or not bring companions; participants arriving with companions
are assigned to the social condition and those arriving alone to the
solo condition.

\subsection{Participants}

Participants will be recruited through convenience sampling: each team
member will invite friends and family to the museum installation.
A target of 12--16 participants is sought, consistent with prior
gesture usability studies using within-subjects designs and sufficient
to detect medium-sized effects on subjective workload
($d \approx 0.5$, power $= 0.80$, $\alpha = .05$). Participants will
be screened to exclude individuals with upper-limb mobility impairments.
No prior experience with gesture-based systems is required; naive users
represent the target museum visitor population.

Demographic information (age range, technology familiarity on a
5-point scale, museum visit frequency) will be collected via a
pre-study questionnaire. Informed consent will be obtained before
participation.

\subsection{Tasks and Procedure}

Each participant completes a structured sequence of six phases:
(1)~a 2-minute introduction and gesture demonstration;
(2)~a 3-minute free exploration period;
(3)~a 10-minute structured task block of 15 directed tasks (e.g.,
\textit{``Navigate to the China exhibition,''} \textit{``Add this
exhibit to your favorites''});
(4)~a silent condition switch (threshold changed in the Python
pipeline without informing the participant);
(5)~a second 10-minute structured task block with the same 15 tasks
in a new random order;
(6)~a 10-minute post-task questionnaire and semi-structured interview.
Total session duration is approximately 40~minutes.

Participants are encouraged to think aloud throughout the task blocks.
Sessions are video-recorded; the facilitator simultaneously takes
structured observational notes.

\subsection{Measures}

\subsubsection{Quantitative}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Task completion rate}: Whether each task is completed
          within 30~seconds; failures are coded if the participant
          abandons the task or exceeds the time limit.
    \item \textbf{Time-on-task (ToT)}: Elapsed seconds from task
          presentation to successful gesture recognition, measured from
          video timestamps.
    \item \textbf{Gesture attempt count}: Number of gesture attempts
          required per task ($1 = $ first-attempt success; $2+ = $
          retry required).
    \item \textbf{Recognition error rate}: Number of misrecognition
          events (false positives + false negatives) per task block,
          extracted from the Python pipeline console log.
    \item \textbf{NASA-TLX~\cite{hart1988}}: Six subscales (mental
          demand, physical demand, temporal demand, performance, effort,
          frustration) rated on 20-point scales, yielding a composite
          workload score per condition.
    \item \textbf{Confidence rating}: A single 7-point Likert item
          (\textit{``I felt confident that my gestures were understood
          by the system''}) collected after each task block.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Qualitative}
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,topsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Gesture adaptation behavior}: Coded from video
          using a scheme adapted from Rico and Brewster~\cite{rico2010}:
          \textsc{repeat}, \textsc{amplify}, \textsc{switch}, and
          \textsc{seek-help}.
    \item \textbf{Verbal frustration indicators}: Utterances
          categorized as neutral, mild (\textit{``that didn't
          work''}), or strong (raised voice, negative evaluation).
    \item \textbf{Social behavior}: Instances of looking at
          companions, receiving verbal coaching, laughing or smiling,
          and explicitly inviting companions to observe.
    \item \textbf{Interview responses}: Analyzed via reflexive
          thematic analysis~\cite{braun2006} to identify themes
          related to ease of use, pleasure, embarrassment, and
          comparison with familiar interaction modalities.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Analysis Plan}

Quantitative data will be analyzed using paired-samples $t$-tests (or
Wilcoxon signed-rank tests if normality is violated) to compare the two
algorithm conditions on all performance and experience measures. Effect
sizes will be reported as Cohen's $d$. For the between-subjects social
context comparison, independent-samples $t$-tests will be used.

Qualitative video data will be coded independently by two researchers;
intercoder reliability will be assessed using Cohen's $\kappa$.
Discrepancies will be resolved through discussion. Interview transcripts
will be analyzed inductively, with codes linked to each of the five
research questions.

\section{Semi-Structured Interview Protocol}
\label{app:interview}

\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep,topsep=4pt]
    \item Which gesture felt most natural to perform? Which felt most
          awkward?
    \item Describe what you were thinking or feeling when the system
          did not respond to your gesture. What did you do next?
    \item Did you change the way you performed gestures over the
          session? If so, how and why?
    \item Were you aware of other people in the room while you were
          interacting? How did their presence affect your use of the
          system?
    \item How would you compare this interaction style to a touchscreen
          or voice-command system in a museum context?
    \item Is there a gesture you tried that you expected to work but
          did not?
    \item Overall, how would you describe the experience of interacting
          with the museum in this way?
\end{enumerate}
